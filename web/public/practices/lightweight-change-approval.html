Lightweight Change Approval

Software Engineering Best Practice Lightweight Change Approval emphasizes the use of a lightweight change approval process, which has been shown through research to yield superior IT performance compared to external change approval boards (CABs).

Key aspects covered by the practice

  • Lightweight Process: The change approval process is designed to be streamlined and efficient, avoiding unnecessary overhead. It aims to strike a balance between ensuring change quality and maintaining a smooth workflow.
  • Peer Review: The practice advocates for a peer review-based change approval process. This can be achieved through techniques such as pair programming or intrateam code review. Peer review leverages the expertise within the development team to assess and validate changes.
  • Superior IT Performance: Research findings suggest that lightweight change approval processes based on peer review result in improved IT performance. By utilizing peer review, issues and defects can be detected earlier, leading to higher-quality software.

Risks of not implementing Practice

Risk NumberTitleDescription
LCAR-1Bottlenecked Decision-MakingLong approval processes can introduce bottlenecks in decision-making, leading to delays in approving and implementing changes. Examples include CABs, Change Control Boards, or formal change review committees.
LCAR-2Increased Time-to-MarketComplex and lengthy approval processes can result in increased time-to-market, hindering the ability to respond quickly to customer needs and market demands.
LCAR-3Decreased AgilityRigid long approval processes restrict agility and hinder the ability to iterate and adapt to evolving requirements and feedback. Examples include extensive change request forms, multi-stage approval hierarchies, or bureaucratic workflows.
LCAR-4Inefficient Resource UtilizationLong approval processes involving numerous stakeholders lead to inefficient utilization of resources, including personnel and time. Examples include extensive documentation requirements, excessive meetings, or lengthy review cycles.
LCAR-5Loss of Developer ProductivityLengthy approval processes impact developer productivity, as they must wait for approvals instead of focusing on coding and delivering value. Examples include excessive wait times for approvals, complex change request processes, or rigid change control procedures.
LCAR-6Increased Complexity and OverheadComplex approval processes introduce additional administrative overhead and increase the complexity of change management. Examples include convoluted change request forms, intricate decision-making frameworks, or excessive documentation requirements.
LCAR-7Higher Risk of Communication BreakdownsWith numerous stakeholders involved in long approval processes, there is a higher risk of miscommunication, misunderstandings, and information gaps. Examples include distributed decision-making, lack of clarity in approval criteria, or inadequate communication channels.
LCAR-8Limited Contextual KnowledgeLong approval processes may lack in-depth contextual knowledge of the system, resulting in suboptimal decisions regarding change approvals. Examples include inadequate understanding of system dependencies, insufficient technical expertise, or limited visibility into the impact of changes.
LCAR-9Ineffective Risk ManagementComplex approval processes can lead to inadequate risk management, as detailed analysis and evaluation may be bypassed or overlooked. Examples include rushed assessments, incomplete risk mitigation strategies, or insufficient consideration of potential impacts.
LCAR-10Resistance to ChangeLong approval processes can foster a culture of resistance to change, making it challenging to introduce innovative ideas and improvements in a timely manner. Examples include conservative decision-making tendencies, resistance to change from stakeholders, or organizational reluctance to adopt new practices.
